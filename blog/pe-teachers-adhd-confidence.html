Building Teacher Confidence in Primary PE

Supporting pupils with ADHD in PE is not only about knowing which strategies to use. It is also about developing the confidence to adapt, respond, and make thoughtful decisions in fast-moving lessons.

Supporting pupils with ADHD in PE

Supporting pupils with ADHD is an expected part of inclusive practice in schools (Wilson et al., 2024). PE gives pupils opportunities to learn through movement, interact with others, and develop social skills (Rudd et al., 2021; Vickerman & Maher, 2017). It can also help pupils experience success and engage positively with physical activity (Taylor et al., 2019).

For pupils with ADHD, however, PE can present particular challenges, especially when lessons involve waiting, following instructions, regulating emotions, or managing competition and busy environments (Villa-de Gregorio et al., 2023). Teachers often need to make quick pedagogical decisions about when to step in, how to adapt an activity, and how to support individual pupils while keeping the lesson moving. This reflects the dynamic and responsive nature of PE described in ecological approaches to teaching (Rudd et al., 2021).

Recent research suggests that teachers are more likely to use supportive strategies when they believe those strategies will be effective. In a survey of 336 teachers, Szep et al. (2021) found that training, school-related factors, and teachers' beliefs about what works were associated with the use of supportive strategies. Teachers' knowledge of ADHD and access to relevant professional learning also appear to play an important role in shaping practice (Akdag, 2023; Ward et al., 2021).

Much of the existing research focuses on classroom settings, with less attention given to how these challenges play out in dynamic PE environments. In PE, supporting pupils with ADHD is not only about knowing what strategies to use. It is also about having the confidence to apply those strategies flexibly in practice.

My research builds on this by exploring how nine primary PE teachers developed confidence in their pedagogical decisions through experience, reflection, and support from the wider school system.

How pedagogical confidence develops

Teachers often said their confidence grew through experience. Rather than relying only on formal training, they became more confident by repeatedly teaching pupils with ADHD and learning from those experiences over time.

This process was not always straightforward. Teachers described using trial and error, reflecting on lessons, and making changes based on what seemed to help individual pupils. When lessons went well, this gave them reassurance that their approach was working. When things were more difficult, they had to rethink, adapt, and try again.

Confidence seemed to develop through learning how to respond flexibly to changing situations, rather than through becoming more certain or simply more experienced. Teachers also became more confident as they got to know pupils better, understanding how individuals responded to different tasks, instructions, and lesson environments.

Learning from others was also important. Teachers described watching colleagues, sharing ideas, and drawing on staff support. These interactions helped them build practical knowledge, reflect on their teaching, and feel more able to adapt their practice.

Why school context matters

Experience helped teachers build confidence, but the school context also shaped how well they could support pupils with ADHD in PE.

Teaching assistants were often described as essential. When support staff were available and understood pupils' needs, teachers felt better able to provide targeted support without disrupting the rest of the lesson.

Class size also mattered. Larger groups made it harder to give individual attention, notice problems early, and make quick adjustments during lessons. Teachers also explained that clear behaviour expectations and support across the wider school made it easier to respond consistently when challenges arose.

Professional development was another important factor. Many teachers felt that most training was designed for classroom settings and did not reflect the fast-moving, practical nature of PE. As a result, much of their learning came from experience, observation, and working with colleagues.

Practical Strategies to Support Pupils with ADHD

In PE, support often comes through practical teaching decisions made in the moment. Teachers need to think carefully about how they design tasks, use space, manage transitions, and keep pupils actively involved in movement.

  1. Keep pupils moving and meaningfully involved. Reducing waiting time and giving each pupil a clear role can support attention and reduce off-task behaviour.
  2. Adapt tasks, space, and groupings in the moment. Changing activities, team sizes, or rules can help manage overwhelm and keep pupils engaged.
  3. Show, do not just tell. Demonstrations and visual cues can reduce reliance on verbal instructions, which some pupils with ADHD may find harder to process and remember.
  4. Reflect on what worked in the activity. Reflection helps teachers build better strategies over time, especially when pupil responses change from lesson to lesson.
  5. Learn from PE colleagues and support staff. Sharing strategies gives teachers practical ways to support different needs when formal training is limited.
  6. Use support staff strategically in active lessons. Extra adult support can help pupils manage emotions, frustration, and re-entry into activities.
  7. Build structure into movement, space, and transitions. Clear routines, smaller groups, and predictable transitions help pupils understand what is expected.
  8. Advocate for PE-specific CPD. Training should reflect the practical demands of PE, rather than relying only on classroom-based approaches.

So what does this mean?

Supporting pupils with ADHD in PE can be complex and depends on the situation. Teachers in this research also highlighted the positive side of PE, describing how some pupils thrived when lessons were adapted to help them experience success.

Being confident as a teacher does not mean having all the answers. It means being able to respond flexibly to changing, sometimes unpredictable situations. That confidence grows with experience, but it becomes stronger when teachers have the right support around them.

Inclusive PE is not the responsibility of individual teachers alone. Schools also have a role in creating the conditions that help teachers feel supported, keep learning, and respond confidently to pupils' needs. When that happens, PE is more likely to become an inclusive and positive space for all pupils.
